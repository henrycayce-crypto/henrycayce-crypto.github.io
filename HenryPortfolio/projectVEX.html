Over the course of the 2024–25 VEX Robotics season, our team designed, built, and iteratively refined a competitive robot capable of scoring rings on mobile goals, clamping field elements, and operating autonomously — ultimately qualifying for the VEX Robotics World Championship.

Final Build

The final robot represented the culmination of three major build iterations and dozens of hours of prototyping, testing, and refinement. Every challenge encountered throughout the season directly informed the final design — resulting in a robot that was mechanically robust, strategically capable, and competition-ready.

Key achievements included an omnidirectional pneumatic clamp capable of securing the octagonal base of mobile goals regardless of approach orientation. Autonomous navigation relied on a precisely tuned vertical odometry system. The ring intake was powered by a dual-motor chain connected via a unified chain route to flex wheels. A wallstake scoring mechanism was integrated with a compact motor concealed behind the battery.

  • ClampOmnidirectional pneumatic — fits any goal orientation
  • AutonomousVertical odometry tracking
  • Ring IntakeDual-motor chain with flex wheels

Build Process

Iteration 01

First Build — Establishing the Foundation

The first iteration established the core mechanical framework for the season. We built a six-motor flat drivetrain operating at 600 RPM, connected directly to gears at a 48:36 ratio — yielding an effective drivetrain speed of 450 RPM. This configuration provided a reliable and responsive base for field navigation.

The primary focus of this iteration was prototyping and tuning the hook chain mechanism — a key system responsible for launching rings onto the mobile goals. We experimented with the height and geometry of the hook to optimize ring placement consistency across varying field positions.

  • Drivetrain6-motor flat, 600 RPM
  • Gear Ratio48:36 → 450 RPM effective
  • Key MechanismHook chain for ring scoring
First build — flat 6-motor drivetrain with hook chain prototype

CAD Redesign

CAD-Driven Redesign

Following our state qualification, we undertook a full redesign using the VEX V5 Onshape CAD library — allowing us to model and validate the new architecture before committing to a physical build, significantly reducing iteration time and material waste.

The core structural change was a transition to a stacked drivetrain, with the rear two motors mounted vertically to reduce the robot's footprint and free up space for the ring intake system. The chain motors were moved inside the chain loop for better protection and torque distribution.

  • Design ToolVEX V5 Onshape CAD Library
  • DrivetrainStacked rear motors — reduced profile
  • Chain Motors2× 600 RPM — internal to chain loop
Onshape CAD model — stacked drivetrain redesign across three views

Iteration 02

Physical Rebuild — Stacked Drivetrain

With the CAD model validated, we executed the physical rebuild — introducing the stacked drivetrain, expanded ring intake, and repositioned chain motors. This configuration reduced the robot's overall profile while improving protection and torque distribution across the chain system.

This was the robot we used to qualify for State. In the weeks following our first competition, we continued refining the hook mechanism and intake geometry before the State tournament.

  • DrivetrainStacked rear motors — smaller profile
  • Chain MotorsRepositioned inside loop — protected
  • IntakeExpanded ring intake space

Technical Challenges

Problem

Hook Chain Disrupting Odometry

The hook chain mechanism's vibration and motion were interfering with the vertical odometry wheel — the sole sensor responsible for tracking the robot's position during autonomous mode. This caused significant positional drift, making reliable autonomous routines impossible.

Solution

Odometry Compaction & Hook Redesign

We redesigned and compacted the odometry mounting system to mechanically isolate it from chain-induced vibration. Simultaneously, we prototyped a new hook geometry to ensure consistent ring placement — requiring multiple iterations to validate ring behavior across varying approach angles.

Problem

Intake Ramp Inconsistency

The intake ramp struggled with ring deflection and inconsistent loading at speed. The material and geometry of the ramp needed to balance friction, rigidity, and responsiveness to handle rings cleanly without jamming.

Solution

Acetal Delrin & Elastic Resistance

We machined the intake ramp from Acetal Delrin — a high-performance engineering plastic with an exceptionally low coefficient of friction. An elastic resistance mechanism was integrated into the intake, allowing it to deflect upward on contact and snap back immediately, improving reliability at competition speeds.

What I Learned

Engineering is iterative.
Every failure is a design constraint.

01

CAD Before You Build

Using Onshape to model the rebuild before cutting metal saved significant time and revealed spatial conflicts — like motor placement — that would have been costly to discover on the physical robot.

02

Material Selection Matters

Switching to Acetal Delrin was a small change with a large impact. Choosing the right material for high-friction, high-speed components is as important as the mechanical design itself.

03

Systems Interact

The hook chain disrupting the odometry wheel was a lesson in systems thinking — a change in one subsystem can create unexpected failures in another. Designing with the full system in mind is critical in competitive robotics.
